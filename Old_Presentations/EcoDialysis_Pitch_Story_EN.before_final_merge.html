Slide 5 · Measurement architecture

How do we measure wasted water?

We meter the dialysis water system at four points and convert flows into liters per session, euros, and reuse potential.
1) RO feedwater meter
2) RO permeate meter
3) RO reject meter
4) Reused-water outlet meter
Optional: tank level, conductivity, temperature, pump runtime
Cross-check: session counts, machine logs, water bills
Core formulas:
  • RO reject = feedwater - permeate
  • L/session = monthly reject volume / monthly sessions
  • capture rate = reused volume / RO reject volume
  • substitution rate = reused volume / non-clinical demand
  • savings = m3 reused × water + sewer tariff
  • carbon note = water-supply-only, not full pathway carbon

Slide 6 · Reuse map

What can be reused?

Not back to the patient. Not back to dialysis. Only to non-clinical uses, selected site by site. This is non-clinical reuse only.
Green · usually strongest
  • toilets
  • technical cleaning where allowed
  • irrigation
  • cooling/technical systems where compatible
  • boilers/steam/laundry only if quality + regulation fit
Orange · site-specific
  • sterilizers
  • cooling towers
  • laundry
  • boilers
  • floor cleaning
  • outdoor washing
Red · not current EcoDialysis deployment
  • dialysis water production
  • patient-contact pathways
  • spent dialysate reuse
  • mixed wastewater reuse without advanced validation
Slide 7 · Regulation

Current law vs new framework

The new EICH framework opens a pathway, but every hospital project still needs a site-specific safety and authorization logic.
Before
  • Fragmented reuse logic
  • Hard to standardize hospital projects
Now
  • EICH framework opens non-potable pathway under conditions
  • Sensitive establishments have strict safeguards
Still required
  • ARS / prefecture validation depending on case
  • separated network
  • backflow prevention
  • signage
  • sanitary logbook
  • monitoring + maintenance
  • no patient-contact reuse

Legal details in appendix and always to verify with ARS/legal counsel. Gov

Slide 8 · Playbooks

site-specific playbooks

EcoDialysis is needed because every site has different machines, RO systems, layout, quality profile, demand profile, and legal pathway.
  1. Site type? CHU / large CH / private center / medium hospital / small unit.
  2. Water system? old RO / modern RO / high-recovery RO / central loop.
  3. Demand nearby? toilets / laundry / boiler / cooling / irrigation / cleaning.
  4. Quality profile? conductivity / microbiology / salinity / storage risk.
  5. Legal route? simple / site-specific / high complexity.
  6. Playbook output? measure only / reduce first / toilets reuse / technical reuse / design-in new-build.
Slide 9 · Proof

Has anyone already done this?

Yes. Dialysis water reuse is already implemented internationally, but the best use case depends on the site.
Canterbury
800 L/h recovered; around £7,500/year savings.
Lesson: robust toilet reuse baseline.
Bristol
13,140 m3/year; >£15,000/year by steam/boiler route.
Lesson: technical route can beat complex retrofit.
Ashford
Design-in pathway with much lower capex than retrofit.
Lesson: design stage matters.
Bendigo
Around 1.7 ML/year.
Lesson: replicable public-hospital model.
Queen Elizabeth
Around 1.5-2 ML/year.
Lesson: higher-complexity governance route.
Barwon
Technical reuse examples (sterilizer/cooling contexts).
Lesson: demand map drives value.
ANZSN
>160,000 L water; 6.4 MWh; 720 L citric solution avoided; $2,400 savings.
Lesson: low-capex process wins first.
Spain + Australian modern vs old RO
Operations affect water+energy (Spain). 357 L + 3.1 kWh vs 548 L + 7.2 kWh per treatment.
Lesson: schedule + equipment matter.

Slide 11 · Business model

Who pays and why?

Hospitals pay because water resilience is operational risk management, not optional sustainability.
  • diagnostic audit fee
  • feasibility study
  • implementation support
  • SaaS monitoring
  • success fee on verified savings
  • supplier/integrator partnership
  • GHT/network package
  • subsidy application support where eligible
CHU / CHRU: resilience + technical capacity
Large CH / GHT: standardization benefit
Private network: cost + replicability

Not claimed: guaranteed CNAM reimbursement, guaranteed funding, guaranteed payback.

What should we do first?

Start with 3 pilots, not national rollout.
