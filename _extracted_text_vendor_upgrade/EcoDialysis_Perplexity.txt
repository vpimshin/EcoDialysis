 Slide 1: Problem – Dialysis Wastes High‑Quality Water Headline: Hospital dialysis in France discards millions of liters of high‑grade water that could safely replace non‑potable uses. France has about 51,700 dialysis patients; over half of all renal replacement patients are on dialysis, with 77% in non‑autonomous in‑center/UDM units, i.e. mainly hospital or satellite facilities. [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] A typical haemodialysis session uses around 400–500 L of mains water, of which 50–70% is rejected by the reverse‑osmosis (RO) unit as clean greywater that is normally sent to drain. [6] [7] [8] [9] Large, multi‑station dialysis centers in university hospitals (32 CHU/CHRU) and big private clinics are few in number but treat a large fraction of patients, making them the natural first adopters of water‑reuse infrastructure. [10] [11] [12] Case studies in European hospitals show RO‑reject recovery systems can save 3,000–5,000 m³ of water per year per unit with payback times of roughly 1–3.5 years, using the water for toilet flushing, laundry, or horticulture. [13] [9] [14] France’s drinking water production has an estimated carbon footprint of roughly 0.23–0.27 kgCO₂e per m³, so reusing RO‑reject instead of new potable water avoids both water abstraction and associated emissions. [15] Slide 2: France Market Size and Segmentation Key facts (system size and concentration) At end‑2024, France had 2,965 health establishments: 1,330 public hospitals, 655 private non‑profit establishments and 980 private for‑profit clinics. [16] In 2022, 93,084 patients were on renal replacement therapy; 55.5% were on dialysis (≈51,700 patients), the rest living with a functioning graft. [4] [5] Modality split from the REIN registry/France Rein shows 51.7% of dialysis patients in in‑center hospital HD, 26% in medicalized dialysis units (UDM), and the remainder in self‑dialysis, home HD (~1.5%), and peritoneal dialysis. [2] [3] [1] Where activity is concentrated (for water‑reuse potential) Dialysis in France is delivered in about 1,100–1,200 units; a network analysis identified 1,166 dialysis units in 2018, organized into ~160 regional “communities” of care. [12] Large in‑center HD and UDM facilities (often CHU/large CH plus big private dialysis centers) run many stations and multiple shifts, so they deliver far more sessions per site than small satellite/self‑dialysis units. [11] [2] [12] Given that non‑autonomous in‑center/UDM accounts for ~77% of dialysis patients, most water‑reuse potential is concentrated in a few hundred large or medium‑sized hospital‑based units rather than in dispersed home or small self‑dialysis facilities. Slide 3: Technology Options – Ranked and Scored Context (why water reuse is realistic but bounded) Haemodialysis uses treated water to prepare dialysate; RO systems reject 50–70% of incoming water, which never contacts blood or dialysate and is therefore relatively clean (low microbial risk, but not potable‑grade). [18] [7] [6] International experience shows that capturing RO‑reject and routing it to grey‑water uses (toilets, irrigation, some cleaning/laundry) is technically straightforward when separated from potable circuits and designed with estates/engineering teams. [19] [9] [14] [13] More ambitious concepts—reuse of spent dialysate or heat recovery—are discussed in circular‑economy nephrology papers but have limited real‑world deployment and need further validation. [20] [14] [21] Ranking and simple scoring (1–5; 5 = best) Scores are expert judgement based on current literature and case studies; they indicate relative positioning, not precise metrics. Capture and reuse of RO‑reject water for toilet flushing and irrigation (near‑term) How it works: RO‑reject is piped from dialysis water plant to a storage tank, then pumped to a separate grey‑water network feeding toilet cisterns and/or landscape irrigation; bypass to drain during disinfection or tank overflow. [9] [14] [13] Maturity: Proven in multiple hospitals (UK, Ireland, Malaysia) for 10+ years, with documented volumes, costs and payback. [14] [9] Safety: Low infection risk because water never re‑enters clinical circuits or potable water; main concerns are cross‑connection control and legionella management in storage/distribution. [20] [9] [14] Fit: Best for large and medium hospitals with big dialysis units and sizable toilet/irrigation demand. Scores: Impact 5 / Ease 4 / Safety 4 / Cost 4 / Time‑to‑pilot 5. RO‑reject reuse for cleaning, technical uses, and selected laundry processes (near‑ to mid‑term) How it works: As above, but water is also used for floor cleaning, certain washer‑disinfectors, cooling towers, or pre‑wash cycles where hardness and conductivity are acceptable. [19] [14] [20] Maturity: Some case reports (e.g. reject feeding laundries, hot‑water systems) but less standardized; needs site‑specific engineering and compatibility checks with equipment. [14] [19] Safety: Requires risk assessment for biofilm/scale; must not be used where drinking‑water standards are required or where aerosols pose infection risk without adequate treatment. [20] [14] Fit: Large hospitals with technical services and multiple non‑clinical water demands; less suitable for small sites. Scores: Impact 4 / Ease 3 / Safety 3 / Cost 3 / Time‑to‑pilot 3. Optimizing RO efficiency and dialysis water demand (complementary, near‑term) How it works: Use higher‑efficiency RO units, adjust recovery rate, minimize rinse cycles, and optimize dialysate flow; larger centers already show lower water use per session when plants are well‑chosen and run continuously. [18] [11] [6] Maturity: Well described in environmental nephrology reviews; many manufacturers already offer higher‑recovery systems and smart controls. [7] [11] [6] Safety: Must not compromise water quality; changes are usually within manufacturer specifications and under existing standards. [11] [18] Fit: All hospital sizes; strongest gains in large centers. Scores: Impact 3 / Ease 4 / Safety 4 / Cost 4 / Time‑to‑pilot 4. Treatment and reuse of mixed haemodialysis wastewater (spent dialysate + other streams) for irrigation or technical uses (mid‑term) How it works: Collect total haemodialysis wastewater, treat via coagulation, sedimentation and ultrafiltration, then reuse for irrigation or other non‑potable uses; RO‑reject alone can also be used where salinity permits. [22] [19] Evidence: An Iranian hospital study showed CFS+UF treatment could meet reuse criteria for several parameters, but high salinity and organic load limited direct irrigation without focusing specifically on RO‑reject. [22] [19] Safety/complexity: Higher; involves handling potentially contaminated effluent, more complex treatment trains, and stricter monitoring; likely too complex for initial pilots in France. Fit: Larger centers with ambitious sustainability programs; probably not first wave. Scores: Impact 4 / Ease 2 / Safety 2 / Cost 2 / Time‑to‑pilot 2. Reuse of spent dialysate and thermal energy recovery (long‑term) How it works: Concepts include purifying spent dialysate for toilet flushing or other uses and installing heat exchangers to recover thermal energy from warm effluent. [21] [14] [20] Evidence: Mostly conceptual and small‑scale; the 2024 circular‑economy point‑of‑view paper lists these as future opportunities, not standard practice; no large hospital‑wide deployments reported. [21] [14] [20] Safety/complexity: High, as dialysate effluent contains blood traces, solutes and potentially pathogens; any reuse needs robust treatment, validation and regulatory approval. Fit: Research centers or flagship university hospitals; not ready for mainstream deployment. Scores: Impact 5 / Ease 1 / Safety 1 / Cost 1 / Time‑to‑pilot 1. Slide 4: Evidence – Impact, Savings, and Uncertainty Water consumption and reject volumes (well‑supported) Multiple technical and clinical sources converge on ~400–500 L of mains water per standard 4‑hour HD session, with RO systems rejecting roughly 50–70% of feed water; that implies 200–350 L of RO‑reject per session. [8] [6] [7] [9] [18] The 2025 Clinical Kidney Journal environmental impact study of 20 HD centers reported average water consumption of about 600 ± 200 L per session, with larger centers using less water per session than smaller ones. [23] [11] A global review estimated total HD water use at 265 million m³/year for one million hemodialyzed patients, with about two‑thirds as RO‑reject, consistent with ~0.5 m³ of total water per session. [6] [18] Case studies of RO‑reject reuse (strong for feasibility and economics, mostly outside France) The Canterbury dialysis unit (UK) installed a system in 1999 that recovers about 800 L/h of RO‑reject, piping it to a grey‑water tank to flush hospital toilets; capital cost was ~£15,000, with annual water/sewer savings of ~£7,500 and payback in ~2 years. [13] [9] [14] Other hospitals (e.g. Countess of Chester, Lister, Maidstone, Sultan Abdul Halim in Malaysia) report recovered volumes of 1,460–5,240 m³/year, investment costs around 6,000–14,000 (USD equivalent) and payback between ~12 and 42 months. [14] A case study from Saveh hospital in Iran showed that focusing on RO‑reject alone (2,300 L/d) could irrigate ~287.5 m² of hospital green space, whereas fully treated mixed haemodialysis wastewater remained too saline for direct irrigation, reinforcing the focus on RO‑reject as the most practical fraction. [19] [22] Order‑of‑magnitude impact for a French large hospital (reasoned estimate) Assumptions clearly stated: 100 chronic in‑center/UDM patients in a large hospital; 3 sessions/week; 156 sessions/patient/year → 15,600 sessions/year. 270 L RO‑reject/session as a conservative mid‑point within the 200–350 L range. [7] [8] [9] [6] Water savings: 15,600 × 0.27 m³ ≈ 4,200 m³/year of potable water avoided if all RO‑reject is reused for toilets/irrigation (assumption based on linear scaling from international case studies). [9] [14] CO₂e savings: Using 0.23–0.27 kgCO₂e/m³ for water supply in France, this corresponds to roughly 1.0–1.1 tCO₂e avoided per hospital‑year, excluding wastewater treatment emissions (estimate extrapolated from drinking‑water CF data). [15] Financial savings: If combined water and sewer tariffs are on the order of a few €/m³ (typical of European urban tariffs; assumption, no France‑specific figure used), 4,200 m³/year implies low five‑figure euro savings per year; UK sites realized ~£7,500/year savings at similar scales. [9] [14] Scaling to the French system (illustrative, not precise) If 100–150 large and medium hospitals implemented RO‑reject reuse achieving 3,000–5,000 m³/year each (range of published case studies), total savings would be 300,000–750,000 m³/year, corresponding to roughly 70–200 tCO₂e/year from water supply alone. [15] [9] [14] These values are modest at national scale but material at hospital level and symbolically important for “green nephrology”; they also come with no clinical trade‑off when kept strictly in non‑clinical circuits. [11] [20] [14] Confidence levels (for your pitch) High confidence: Dialysis water use per session; fraction of RO‑reject; total dialysis population and modality split; technical feasibility and payback (1–3.5 years) of RO‑reject reuse for toilets/irrigation in large centers; relative environmental benefit of focusing on in‑center/UDM units. [5] [2] [18] [6] [11] [9] [14] Moderate confidence: Extrapolated per‑hospital French water/CO₂ savings (depends on exact patient load, water tariffs, and RO settings); assumption that 100–150 hospitals represent most of the economic potential. Low confidence / exploratory: Large‑scale reuse of spent dialysate and thermal energy in French hospitals; reuse of fully treated haemodialysis wastewater for sensitive uses beyond irrigation/technical processes. Slide 6: Business Model and Equity Who pays, who benefits, who operates? Buyer / decision‑maker: Hospital management (public CHU/CH and private dialysis groups), typically via estates/engineering, within regional hospital groups (GHT) or private chains; they control capex budgets and sustainability objectives. [24] [16] [11] User / operator: Dialysis unit staff (as primary water producers) and hospital technical services who maintain RO plants, storage tanks, pumps and grey‑water networks. [11] [9] [14] Beneficiary: Direct: Hospital (reduced water and sewer bills, better environmental reporting), dialysis services (green nephrology reputation), and hospital groups aiming at decarbonization targets. [20] [14] [11] Indirect: Local water utilities and communities (reduced demand on potable water and wastewater treatment), and patients who may value sustainable care. [18] [6] [20] Partner: Water/energy utilities and engineering firms that design/install water‑reuse systems. Medical device/RO manufacturers offering higher‑recovery systems and integration with sustainability modules. [7] [11] Regional health authorities (ARS) and Agence de la transition écologique for grants or inclusion in climate plans. [11] [20] Maintainer: Hospital estates/biomedical engineering teams, possibly with maintenance contracts to specialized water‑treatment providers. [19] [9] [14] Economic value proposition For large centers, RO‑reject reuse is essentially a utility‑savings project : capex for tanks, piping, pumps and controls (tens of thousands of euros) repaid over a few years via avoided potable water and sewer costs, as suggested by international payback data (1–3.5 years) and French water‑carbon intensities. [15] [9] [14] Additional value comes from regulatory and reputational benefits: alignment with French and EU climate policies, contributions to hospital‑level sustainability KPIs, and demonstration of responsible resource use in a highly resource‑intensive therapy. [14] [20] [11] Procurement and implementation logic Start with performance‑based procurement : suppliers commit to delivering a defined amount of recoverable water (m³/year) and providing remote monitoring of flows and quality, while hospitals retain ownership of savings; options for shared‑savings contracts can be explored but require legal work. Use framework contracts at GHT or private‑group level to standardize technical specifications, risk assessments, and maintenance models, thus lowering transaction costs for each hospital. [14] [11] Equity and access implications Separated into four dimensions as requested: Environmental efficiency: Focusing on large hospitals maximizes liters saved per euro and per engineering hour; small sites are less efficient targets, which is environmentally logical but risks a perception gap. [11] [14] Clinical feasibility: Because water reuse is strictly non‑clinical (toilets, irrigation, cleaning), there is no direct difference in clinical access or modalities between patients; however, pilots must demonstrate that projects never interfere with water quality for dialysis itself. [18] [20] [11] Social fairness (Paris vs regions, rich vs poor hospitals): Early pilots will naturally be in better‑resourced CHU and big private centers (often urban/Paris‑centric), potentially widening perceived inequalities in “green” infrastructure; this can be mitigated by making all technical designs, protocols and data open and reusable for regional hospitals, and by including at least one non‑Paris regional CHU among first pilots. [10] [12] [11] Geographic equity and feasibility for smaller hospitals: Smaller hospitals and rural UDMs may lack capex and engineering capacity; to avoid exclusion, regional authorities or GHT could co‑finance “template” projects and offer regional engineering support or shared maintenance contracts, enabling scaled‑down implementations where volume justifies it. [12] [11] Design principles to improve, not worsen, access Make all tools (design drawings, risk assessments, monitoring dashboards) openly shareable across hospitals to reduce knowledge asymmetry. Tie funding to inclusion criteria that ensure at least some peripheral/regional hospitals participate in the second wave, once the model is proven in flagship centers. Explicitly track staff workload and maintenance burden in pilots to show that solutions are manageable for smaller teams and to refine designs where they are not. [20] [14] [11] Slide 7: Final Pitch Message (20–30 seconds) “Dialysis keeps people alive, but every session in France wastes hundreds of liters of high‑quality water straight to the drain. By capturing this RO‑reject and reusing it for toilets and irrigation in large hospital dialysis units, we can save thousands of cubic meters of water per hospital each year, cut CO₂ emissions, and reduce utility costs—without touching the clinical circuit.
