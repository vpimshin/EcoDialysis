Water Sustainability Practices in Dialysis
Executive summary
The strongest implemented water-sustainability practices in dialysis today are not the most futuristic
ones. They are the pragmatic measures applied around the dialysis water plant: reusing reverse
osmosis reject water for non-patient-contact purposes, upgrading or optimizing reverse osmosis
systems, avoiding redundant disinfection cycles, and recovering heat from warm effluent. These
measures are already producing quantified results in routine care. UK and Australian case studies
report reject-water reuse ranging from about 1,000 L/week in a small rural unit to 13,140 m³/year in a
children’s hospital; direct cost savings range from £7,500/year at Canterbury to more than £15,000/
year at Bristol, with some projects showing payback in a few years rather than decades. Operational
optimization also matters: a multicentre Spanish study found that centres operating daily used
materially less water and energy per dialysis session than centres opening only three times weekly,
indicating that scheduling and asset utilization are themselves sustainability levers. 
On the technology side, the best current evidence favors “reduce and reuse” rather than full “recycle”
of patient-contact effluent. Newer water plants and machine configurations can materially reduce
resource intensity: a recent Australian comparison reported 357 L of RO water and 3.1 kWh of RO
power per treatment with a modern RO system versus 548 L and 7.2 kWh with an older one.
Manufacturers such as B. Braun and Fresenius Medical Care advertise up to 85% RO water recovery on
newer systems, and Fresenius reports wider deployment of heat disinfection and water metering in
water-stressed clinics. Heat-recovery interventions are also maturing: retrofitted dialysis-machine heat
exchangers in NHS services saved 0.5–0.86 kWh per treatment, while a 2026 simulation/experimental
validation for a four-station clinic projected >15 kWh/session and ~63,200 kWh/year recovered. 
By contrast, closed-loop spent-dialysate regeneration, forward osmosis, and portable sorbent
hemodialysis remain promising but comparatively immature. Sorbent systems can dramatically cut
water demand: the legacy REDY system required about 6 L of untreated water per dialysis, and
NextKidney’s NeoKidney reports 4.5–5 L of dialysate per session with no separate home water
treatment system, but the company also states that the device is not yet for sale and that regulatory
approvals are pending. The clinical safety evidence retrievable in this review is still limited: an AWAK
peritoneal dialysis pilot reported no serious adverse events in 15 patients but did report frequent
abdominal discomfort, underscoring that regenerative approaches are not yet “drop-in” replacements
for conventional dialysis infrastructure. 
The policy picture is mixed. Water-quality regulation for dialysis is already strict and well established;
sustainability regulation and incentives are newer and more fragmented. Current rules mainly act as a 
boundary condition: they protect patient safety, but they also make any reuse that re-enters patient-
contact pathways far more complex than reusing reject water for toilets, boilers, irrigation, or laundry.
The most scalable implementation pattern emerging from the evidence is therefore: measure first,
optimize existing plants and schedules second, reuse non-patient-contact streams third, and
reserve closed-loop patient-contact systems for controlled pilots until the clinical, regulatory, and
economic evidence base is stronger.

Scope and evidence base
This report prioritizes peer-reviewed papers, official hospital and dialysis-service case studies,
manufacturer technical documentation, professional-society guidance, and government or health-
system documents. The primary evidence retrieved clusters around several source types: multicentre
observational studies from Spain and Australia; hospital implementation case studies from the UK and
Australia; official technical or sustainability material from Fresenius Medical Care, B. Braun, DaVita, and
NextKidney; and guidance from WHO, the NSW Agency for Clinical Innovation, ANZSN, ERA/SFNDT,
KDIGO, and ISN GREEN-K. 
A key limitation of the literature is its asymmetry. Quantified evidence is comparatively strong for RO
reject reuse, water-plant upgrades, disinfection scheduling, and heat recovery. It is weaker for 
patient-level clinical outcomes directly attributable to sustainability interventions, and much weaker
still for closed-loop spent-dialysate recycling and portable sorbent hemodialysis. Where sources did
not specify a result, this report records it as unspecified rather than inferring it.

Technical practices
The dialysis water chain has several intervention points, and the evidence suggests that the most robust
gains come from intervening at the RO plant boundary and the effluent/heat boundary, not from
modifying the patient-contact dialysate loop itself. The most mature practices are therefore: increasing
RO efficiency, reusing reject water , right-sizing and scheduling disinfection, and recovering thermal
energy from wastes. More ambitious approaches—direct spent-dialysate purification, forward osmosis
regeneration, and sorbent recirculation—remain largely in pilot, prototype, or early clinical stages.

What the current evidence says
The evidence hierarchy is clear . Reject-water reuse and disinfection optimization are today’s
highest-confidence measures because they avoid patient-contact water pathways, use familiar
engineering, and already have repeated service-level case studies with water , cost, and sometimes
carbon metrics. Heat recovery is a credible next tier: good engineering logic, some implementation
evidence, but still fewer large-scale replications. Portable sorbent systems and spent-dialysate
regeneration offer the largest theoretical water savings, but the present evidence base is still too early
for broad implementation recommendations outside controlled pilots. 
Operational practices
Operational practice is not a side issue in dialysis sustainability; it is often the reason identical
equipment performs very differently across sites. The most consistent operational findings in the
retrieved literature are that higher utilization, fewer redundant cleaning cycles, formal training and
competency systems, continuous monitoring, and good maintenance discipline all reduce resource
intensity without lowering the patient-safety bar .

A second operational lesson is that supply-demand matching matters as much as supply volume.
The Australian handbook emphasizes that RO reject water is generated predictably during the working
day and often matches toilet-flushing demand well, whereas irrigation is seasonal and may need
storage or may not actually substitute potable demand. It therefore recommends evaluating projects by
the percentage of reject water used, not merely the amount generated. 
A third lesson is that monitoring should be institutionalized, not improvised. The Spanish
multicentre study concludes that annual monitoring of environmental key performance indicators is
necessary to detect variation and guide action plans. Fresenius reports automated water meters in
water-stressed U.S. clinics to improve treatment-level visibility and intervention targeting. These are
signs that dialysis sustainability is moving from anecdotal “green projects” toward operational
management. 
Policy drivers and stakeholder landscape
Dialysis sustainability is shaped by two different policy layers. The first is the traditional water-quality
and infection-control layer, which is already mature and non-negotiable. ACI NSW requires written
policies, trained and accredited operators, routine chlorine and post-RO testing, trending, audits,
committee review, and maintenance logs; it also references ISO 13959 for contaminant control. These
rules are the reason why sustainability measures that remain outside the patient-contact water loop
scale more easily than measures that try to re-enter it. 
The second layer is the climate, reporting, procurement, and resilience layer, which is newer . WHO’s
guidance for climate-resilient and environmentally sustainable health facilities explicitly frames water ,
energy, procurement, staff training, and monitoring as central operational tasks. Fresenius Medical
Care’s 2025 sustainability statement is prepared under the EU CSRD/ESRS reporting regime. DaVita cites
the U.S. Health Sector Climate Pledge. ISN’s GREEN-K initiative is explicitly pushing a procurement

Quantified outcomes, geography, and scalability
The measured outcomes retrieved in this review are concentrated in a few geographies. The UK
provides some of the clearest business-case case studies for reject-water reuse and heat exchangers. 
Australia contributes much of the best implementation detail on RO-reject reuse, reuse handbooks,
water-safety governance, and disinfection optimization. Spain contributes the best multicentre
observational data on how plant choices and operations change environmental intensity. The
Netherlands, Singapore, Morocco, and the U.S. are more visible in emerging-device and system-level
innovation than in fully mature scaled deployment.

Geographic distribution and scalability
The highest-scalability practices are the ones that require the smallest change to the regulated
patient-contact pathway: disinfection scheduling, metering, staff training, maintenance, and

specification of better RO plants. Those can often be rolled out service-wide with modest capital
spending and little regulatory novelty. The next tier is RO reject-water reuse, which scales well where a
stable nearby demand exists—especially toilets, laundry, sterilizers, and boilers. Scalability drops when
storage, pumping, or long-distance conveyance is needed, or when local non-potable plumbing
standards become stringent. 
The lowest current scaling readiness belongs to patient-contact closed-loop concepts. Even where the
technical promise is strong, the evidence base is still too concentrated in engineering studies, prototype
brochures, conference abstracts, and small pilots. That does not make them unimportant; it means they
are better treated today as strategic innovation tracks rather than as default infrastructure choices

Best-practice recommendations
Start with measurement, not ideology. Every unit should baseline at least four indicators: water per
treatment, RO reject volume, energy per treatment, and number of disinfection cycles. The Spanish
multicentre study and Fresenius’ meter rollout both point in the same direction: the units that can see
their water and energy use are the ones that can manage it. 
Optimize before you invent. The fastest wins usually come from adjusting schedules, removing
redundant disinfection, renewing aging RO plants, optimizing regeneration frequencies, and fixing
leaks or overuse. These are lower-risk measures than closed-loop recycling and often deliver immediate
water and energy reductions. 
Reuse only the safest streams first. The best near-term default is to prioritize RO reject water and
other non-patient-contact streams. The economic and safety evidence is strongest there. Reserve
patient-contact closed-loop approaches for research or carefully governed pilots until more clinical and
regulatory evidence accumulates. 
Design sustainability into new builds. New unit design should explicitly include reject-water reuse,
storage, meters, and heat recovery opportunities where feasible. The cost gap between Ashford and
Canterbury is a powerful reminder that sustainability is much cheaper when planned before the walls
are closed. 
Match reuse to stable demand. Toilet flushing, sterilizer/cooling water , boilers, and laundry typically
beat irrigation because they provide predictable year-round demand and avoid overbuilding storage.
Project appraisal should focus on utilization rate of recovered water , not on gross reject-water
generation. 
Institutionalize competence and governance. A multidisciplinary water committee, operator
accreditation, annual audits, and a maintenance logbook are not bureaucratic extras; they are the
controls that make sustainability changes safe enough to scale. 
Use procurement strategically. When renewing plants or machines, require evidence on recovery rate,
heat-disinfection capability, telemetry/metering, and compatibility with heat-recovery or reuse
architectures. This aligns with ISN GREEN-K’s explicit procurement strategy and with the direction of
European and corporate sustainability reporting.

Open questions and assumptions
Direct patient safety, infection, and hospitalization outcomes attributable to sustainability measures
remain underreported. Most retrieved sources demonstrate safety through water-quality control
protocols or by keeping reuse outside the patient-contact pathway, rather than through comparative
infection studies. That is a meaningful evidence gap. 
Evidence for closed-loop spent-dialysate recycling, forward osmosis, and portable sorbent
hemodialysis is still not robust enough to support broad deployment advice on its own. The resource
potential is substantial, but the field still needs more peer-reviewed clinical data, better published
lifecycle assessments, and transparent cost comparisons at scale. 
A final practical limitation is that some corporate and manufacturer metrics are company-reported and
sometimes extrapolated, not independently audited at the level of each dialysis session or each site.
They are useful directional signals, but they should not be treated as identical in evidentiary weight to a
fully published multicentre observational study.
