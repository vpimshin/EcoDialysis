Abstract
Healthcare systems are major emitters of greenhouse gases that contribute to climate change. The resulting 
climate crisis harms human and planetary health and increasingly impacts lives on Earth. Because of their status as 
high emitters and their social role in society, healthcare systems have a clear obligation to decarbonise. This study 
investigates how healthcare systems worldwide can lower their greenhouse gas emissions and thereby contribute 
to global climate action. We performed a systematic literature review, searching PubMed, GreenFILE, Business 
Source Complete and CINAHL for relevant empirical studies, non-research papers and grey literature published 
between 1 January 2018 and 28 March 2023. The Greenhouse Gas Protocol applied to healthcare systems guided 
thematic and descriptive analyses to explore interventions targeted at scope 1, 2 and 3 emissions. The PRISMA 
guidelines were followed for reporting and synthesising the results. A total of 742 publications were initially 
identified, 230 of which were included in the systematic literature review, comprising 96 empirical studies, 132 
non-research papers and two governmental reports. Numerous conventional and unique decarbonisation actions 
related to healthcare have been reported in the literature. Eight themes were identified: transportation, anaesthesia, 
energy, travel, supply chain, clinical practices, planetary health literacy and system changes. This study shows the 
abundant potential for healthcare decarbonisation while highlighting key uncertainties around decarbonisation 
in healthcare and providing ambitious recommendations to lower emissions globally. Decarbonisation actions are 
needed at the personal, organisational and system levels. The health workforce is vital in driving transformative 
change but needs to be adequately trained and educated about the importance of low-carbon solutions in 
healthcare. Countries worldwide must exchange knowledge about successful decarbonisation interventions and 
best practices to push global progress. Looking forward, action plans and audits at the local level combined with 
large-scale measures and policies are needed to influence the system as a whole.

Background
Anthropogenic climate change is arguably the most 
pressing global challenge of our time, with far-reach -
ing implications for ecosystems, societies and econo -
mies alike. According to the Global Risks Report 2024, 
global warming and its consequences are among the 
top global risks in the coming years and decades [ 1]. 
Over the past century, human activities have funda -
mentally altered the systemic state of planet Earth, 
most notably the Earth’s atmosphere, leading to a rise 
in global temperatures, shifts in weather patterns and 
numerous intersecting environmental consequences 
[2]. Greenhouse gases trap heat in the Earth’s atmo -
sphere, thereby disrupting the planet’s radiative bal -
ance [ 2]. While natural greenhouse gases 1 are essential 
for climate regulation, human activities significantly 
increase greenhouse gas levels, for example, by burning 
fossil fuels and biomass, land-use changes and indus -
trial processes [ 3].
Six of the nine planetary boundaries which are essen -
tial for regulating the Earth’s stability and resilience, 
have already been transgressed—among them is climate 
change, measured through carbon dioxide concentration 
and energy imbalance at the top of the atmosphere [ 4]. 
The 2023 update of the Planetary Boundaries Framework 
suggests that “Earth is now well outside of the safe oper -
ating space for humanity” [ 4] which may have calami -
tous consequences for all life on Earth since human and

impacts of climate change on human health illustrate the 
need for highly developed healthcare systems. However, 
health sector activities emit greenhouse gases and con -
tribute to the climate crisis, being responsible for nearly

Healthcare systems, meaning “all the organisations, 
institutions, people, resources and actions whose primary 
purpose is to improve, restore or maintain health” [ 11], 
are major contributors to global greenhouse gas emis -
sions and their climate footprint is growing. Health Care 
Without Harm quantified these emissions and reported 
that the global healthcare climate footprint was equal to 
4.4% of global net emissions in 2014, matching the green-
house gas emissions of 514 coal-fired power plants [ 14]. 
The emissions primarily stem from powering hospitals, 
employees, patients and visitors travelling to and from 
healthcare facilities and manufacturing and transporting 
healthcare products [15].
Decarbonisation, which refers to the actions humans 
undertake to cut back greenhouse gas emissions from 
human activities, is crucial for mitigating climate change 
and protecting planetary health. The United Nations 
Environment Programme has urged substantial reduc -
tions in global greenhouse gas emissions, commending a 
45% reduction in the years leading up to 2030 compared 
to emissions projections under current policies in place 
to limit global warming to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial 
levels [ 16], a target set out in the Paris Agreement [ 17]. 
This equals a reduction in global emissions similar to that 
observed during the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-
19) pandemic, where lockdown measures led to a tem -
porary global drop in carbon dioxide emissions between 
4.2% and 7.5% in 2020, relative to 2019 [18].
Climate action and decarbonisation efforts are still 
limited and remain insufficient [ 2]. Hesitancy and inac -
tion have been prevailing in recent decades [ 19]. This 
might be because associated benefits will manifest pre -
dominantly in the long run. Myopic decision-making 
and planning—managing without foresight and valuing 
short-term benefits over long-term rewards—may hinder 
climate action [20]. Waiting for a ‘unicorn technology’ to 
solve the decarbonisation problem, thereby disregarding 
low-carbon options available today, will fail to reach cli -
mate targets [20]. Promptly investing in climate action is 
required to bring about both immediate and long-term 
benefits. The International Energy Agency established 
that more than 40% of climate actions needed to limit 
global warming to 1.5  °C are cost-effective [ 21]. More -
over, health co-benefits are expected to be substantial 
and may greatly outweigh climate mitigation costs while 
preventing millions of people from dying prematurely 
worldwide [ 22]. Health benefits mainly follow from 
reducing air pollution, consuming a healthy and sus -
tainable diet and promoting green and active travel [ 22]. 
Additionally, transitioning towards low-carbon health -
care systems is positively linked with the United Nations 
Sustainable Development Goals while enhancing health 
and well-being [23].
The health sector is clearly responsible for taking 
part in climate action and drastically reducing its foot -
print to limit global warming and prevent numerous

First, because of the high emissions from health-sec -

population. Healthcare institutions and the health work -

their patients and the public [ 24]. Healthcare profession-

have the potential to advocate for climate-friendly behav-

the public and encourage climate action within health -

The Director General of the World Health Organisation 
Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus recognises healthcare sys-
tems’ responsibility by stressing that “as medical profes -
sionals, our commitment is to ‘first, do no harm. ’ Places of 
healing should be leading the way, not contributing to the 
burden of disease” [ 14]. The World Health Organisation 
acknowledges the urgent need to respond to the climate 
crisis by transitioning towards sustainable healthcare. At 
the 26th United Nations Climate Change Conference, 
the World Health Organisation’s member states commit-
ted to developing climate-resilient and sustainable low-
carbon health systems. They founded the Alliance for 
Transformative Action on Climate and Health (ATACH) 
to support member states in implementing their commit-
ments [27].
In summary, anthropogenic climate change poses a 
substantial threat to human and planetary health. Sev -
eral health professionals and scientists are developing 
research on interventions and policies to address the 
consequences of climate change on health. However, 
the notion that healthcare systems substantially con -
tribute to this problem has yet to prevail. Investigating 
how healthcare systems can contribute to countering 
the climate crisis by reducing their climate footprint 
is thus highly relevant for the public, including but not 
limited to patients—the ultimate beneficiaries of health -
care—healthcare providers, manufacturers, public health 
specialists, professionals from related fields as well as 
regional, national, international and supranational deci -
sion-and policymakers.
An appropriate framework for classifying emissions is 
the Greenhouse Gas Protocol. The initiative by the World 
Resources Institute and the World Business Council for 
Sustainable Development classifies emissions into scopes 
1, 2 and 3 and thereby provides global standardised 
frameworks for measuring and managing climate-warm -
ing emissions within the private and public sectors [ 28, 
29]. Health Care Without Harm [ 30] and the National 
Health Service (NHS) England [31] apply the Greenhouse 
Gas Protocol to categorise emissions associated with 
healthcare (Fig.  2). Whereas a healthcare system may 
control greenhouse gas emissions from scopes 1 and 2, 
it has limited control over emissions from scope 3 and, 
therefore, may only be capable of influencing them. Scope 
1 includes direct greenhouse gas emissions from health -
care facilities and owned vehicles, for example, emissions 
from heating (stationary combustion), transportation 
(mobile combustion), waste management (on-site treat -
ment), cooling and anaesthetics (fugitive emissions). 
Scope 2 encompasses indirect greenhouse gas emissions, 
namely, purchased energy. Scope 3 covers greenhouse 
gas emissions outside the direct control of the healthcare 
system, such as off-site waste treatment, business travel, 
employees’ communing to and from healthcare facili -
ties, greenhouse gases from inhalers, electricity lost due 
to transmission and distribution and information and 
communications technology. Finally, indirect greenhouse 
gas emissions with an external supply chain are associ -
ated with, for example, medical devices, food, drugs, 
construction and other purchased goods and services. 
Patient and visitor travel is not explicitly included in the 
Greenhouse Gas Protocol scopes but is nevertheless a 
significant source of greenhouse gas emissions associated 
with healthcare systems.
Some research has been conducted on reducing green -
house gas emissions in healthcare [ 32– 35]. However, 
reviews are often limited in scope and unique solutions 
are overlooked. Most research on reducing greenhouse 
gas emissions in healthcare tends to focus on specific, 
narrow aspects of the system. This could mean concen -
trating on a single element like energy consumption in 
hospitals, waste management or the carbon footprint of 
medical equipment. While these areas are important, the 
fragmented nature of the research means that the find -
ings and recommendations might not account for the 
interconnectedness of various components within the 
healthcare system. This narrow focus leads to piecemeal 
solutions that, while valuable, might miss opportuni -
ties for more systemic and comprehensive decarbonisa -
tion strategies. It also means that the potential synergies 
between different decarbonisation measures are not fully 
explored, which could result in suboptimal outcomes. 
Furthermore, research often fails to explore or even rec -
ognise unique solutions that could significantly reduce 
emissions. These overlooked solutions might involve 
new technologies, novel care delivery models, alternative 
approaches to patient care that challenge the status quo 
or even very simple actions that do not seem to warrant 
mentioning, such as turning off appliances when not in 
use. Actions like these are rarely reported in empirical 
research studies but are no less important in achieving 
healthcare decarbonisation. Additionally, unique solu -
tions can come from transdisciplinary approaches, inte -
grating insights from environmental science, engineering 
and social sciences into healthcare decarbonisation

strategies. However, this kind of cross-disciplinary 
research is still relatively rare.
What is needed is a synthesis of impactful solutions 
to decarbonise healthcare systems, including decarboni -
sation actions reported outside traditional empirical 
research and critical considerations for implementation. 
This scientific paper aims to provide a comprehensive 
overview of the current state of healthcare decarbonisa -
tion action, highlighting conventional and unique mitiga-
tion strategies. By synthesising the latest research in this 
field, we seek to contribute to the growing body of knowl-
edge essential for guiding policy and collective efforts 
to address climate change and safeguard the well-being 
of future generations. This systematic literature review 
answers the following question: What are the potential 
actions and solutions to lower greenhouse gas emissions 
of healthcare systems globally?
Methods
We conducted a systematic literature review with a sub -
sequent thematic and descriptive analysis based on best 
practice guidelines from the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) 
[36] and the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic 
Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) [ 37]. A system -
atic literature review yields several benefits, including 
comprehensive coverage of relevant literature, identifica -
tion of knowledge gaps and recommendations for future 
research to assist in evidence-based decision-making 
[36]. Its methodology is designed to enhance transpar -
ency and reproducibility to generate credibility of the 
findings and to minimise bias since the search is con -
ducted in a standardised manner based on pre-defined 
criteria [36].
Search strategy
An exploratory literature search was conducted on the 
database platform Elton B. Stephens Company (EBSCO) 
and the scientific databases PubMed/Medical Literature 
Analysis and Retrieval System Online (MEDLINE), Web 
of Science Core Collection, Excerpta Medica database 
(Embase) and Scopus. Based on the exploratory litera -
ture search, suitable databases for the aim of this study 
were selected, and search queries were generated. On 
28 March 2023, the literature was searched using the 
PubMed/MEDLINE, GreenFILE, Business Source Com -
plete and the Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied

Article screening process
Following the search, all identified citations were loaded 
into EndNote version 20.5 and duplicates were removed. 
One researcher screened the titles and abstracts for 
assessment against the inclusion criteria for the system -
atic literature review. Potentially relevant records were 
retrieved in full. The full texts of the selected records 
were assessed in detail against the inclusion criteria. Rea -
sons for excluding full-text papers that did not meet the 
inclusion criteria were reported. The search results are 
presented in a PRISMA flow diagram [37].
Study validity assessment
Due to the nature of the systematic literature review 
and the included literature, formal risk of bias and cer -
tainty assessments were not conducted. Nevertheless, as 
expected, the methodologies of the papers included in 
the final review were heterogeneous. Naturally, they dif -
fered in their quality and strengths of evidence. These 
differences were considered accordingly throughout the 
data analysis and discussion of the findings to ensure the 
best possible outcome.
Data coding and extraction strategy
A data extraction sheet was created, containing the 
author and date of publication, type of literature and 
study design, the location and setting of the study, the 
aim of the paper, the decarbonisation action(s) discussed, 
the main findings and further information (if applicable), 
including details about the intervention, success fac -
tors, a short critical appraisal and any other comments 
(see Additional File  2). The data collection and analysis 
were guided by the Greenhouse Gas Protocol applied to 
healthcare (see Fig. 2).
Data synthesis and presentation
Qualitative synthesis (thematic and descriptive analyses) 
was chosen as the mode of analysis since it allows for a 
holistic and in-depth analysis of possible decarbonisa -
tion actions within healthcare. It permits the identifica -
tion of patterns and themes across the literature and the 
exploration of inconsistencies and contradictory findings, 
improves the generalisability of the results by pinpointing 
differences and commonalities across differing popula -
tions and settings and improves rigour and validity. Addi-
tionally, thematic analysis is suitable for analysing large 
datasets [39].
The data analysis followed a multi-step approach. First, 
data from each publication were compiled in tabulated 
form, as proposed by Aveyard [ 40], to facilitate thematic 
data analysis (see Additional File  2). Second, the data 
were analysed deductively by applying the Greenhouse 
Gas Protocol and its pre-defined scopes 1, 2 and 3. Third, 
the literature was analysed inductively—the results were 
categorised into newly developed, more detailed catego -
ries within the Greenhouse Gas Protocol scopes. The 
findings of every publication were grouped according 
to the pre-defined and newly developed categories. The 
results are presented in a table format, as proposed by 
Aveyard [40] (see Additional File 4).
Review findings and discussion
A total of 675 records were identified through the data -
base search, and an additional 67 were identified through 
citation searching. After removing duplicates and records 
that did not meet the inclusion criteria, 230 records were 
included in the systematic literature review. Figure  3 
depicts the PRISMA flow diagram.
A diverse set of literature was included in the final 
review. A total of 96 (41.7%) original studies were 
included in the review, with the most common study 
designs being prospective case or cohort studies, fol -
lowed by modelling studies and life cycle assessment 
studies. While many empirical studies about the decar -
bonisation of healthcare systems were found, 132 (57.4%) 
non-research articles were discovered, of which most 
were non-specified articles and reviews. Additionally, 
two (0.9%) pieces of grey literature were identified via 
citation searching and included as examples of govern -
mental reports outlining decarbonisation actions specifi -
cally targeted to the healthcare system of their respective 
countries, England, United Kingdom [ 31] and the United 
States [42].
Only a minority of studies from this systematic lit -
erature review originated from the Global South, a geo -
graphical area vastly affected by climate change. With 
the Health and Care Act of 2022 coming into effect, the 
NHS was the first health system to legally commit to net 
zero emissions [ 31]. This is reflected by the amount of 
literature from the United Kingdom: more than 45 pub -
lications originated from a single country, approximately 
20% of the literature sample. See Additional File  3 for 
descriptive statistics of the included literature. The data 
extraction sheet can be found in Additional File 2.
Actions to lower direct and indirect greenhouse gas 
emissions from healthcare systems were identified. Fig -

Reducing scope 1 emissions: transportation and 
anaesthesia
Globally, 17% of greenhouse gas emissions emitted 
by healthcare systems globally originate directly from 
healthcare facilities and their vehicles (scope 1) [ 14]. 
Direct greenhouse gas emissions from mobile combus -
tion can be reduced by replacing carbon-emitting vehi -
cles with low-carbon and energy-efficient alternatives. 
For example, carbon and noise-free drones can be used 
for the healthcare supply chain to transport samples 
from surgeries to analysis laboratories [ 43] and to deliver 
emergency medical products to healthcare centres [ 44, 
45]. A practical evaluation by Oakey et al. revealed that 
drone transportation reduces travel distance, travel time 
and carbon dioxide emissions [ 43]. However, due to 
multiple challenges, such as restrictive regulations, high 
operating costs, weather dependency and practical dif -
ficulties in relation to ground characteristics and work -
flows, their widespread use in healthcare systems seems 
infeasible until a solution concerning airspace regulations 
is found and technologies improve in ways that reduce 
operating costs and enable the utilisation of drones even 
during unfavourable weather and in locations with criti -
cal ground characteristics [ 43]. A hybrid supply chain 
management model that includes drones for transport -
ing samples and medical products in selected areas seems 
reasonable. More widespread solutions to decarbonise 
are the transition to a hybrid or 100% electric vehicle fleet 
[45– 64] (which should be powered by renewable energy 
sources; otherwise, emissions are merely displaced), to 
equip ambulances with solar panels [ 57], to power vehi -
cles with biofuels [49] or hydrogen [31], to utilise smaller 
vehicles [54] and to reduce the idling time of healthcare 
facility-owned vehicles [54].
Another way to reduce direct emissions within health -
care systems is to reduce fugitive emissions from anaes -
thesia. The most common halogenated anaesthetic agents 
are desflurane and sevoflurane, which contain hydrofluo -
rocarbons and isoflurane which contains chlorofluoro -
carbons [ 65]. In particular, desflurane has an incredibly 
high global warming potential and should therefore be 
avoided as much as possible. The climate footprint of

operating rooms. In 2023, NHS Scotland announced that 
Scotland would be the first country to ban desflurane 
[99].

Reducing scope 1 and 2 emissions: energy
Greenhouse gas emissions from energy can be direct 
(scope 1) or indirect (scope 2). Direct emissions stem 
from stationary combustion at healthcare facilities and 
indirect emissions stem from purchased electricity, 
steam, heating and cooling. The latter is responsible for 
12% of greenhouse gas emissions emitted from health -
care systems globally [14].
In general, mitigation measures can be grouped into 
energy generation, energy management, energy effi -
ciency and staff behaviour. The first category comprises 
the conversion to renewable energy sources, such as 
solar, wind and water [ 31, 45– 49, 51– 60, 62– 64, 76, 86, 
88, 91, 92, 95, 112, 113, 120– 144] and waste [76]. Renew-
able energy can be purchased or generated directly at 
healthcare facilities, for example, by installing photovol -
taic panels. Installing on-site renewable energy systems 
not only has the benefits of reducing emissions from sta -
tionary combustion and eliminating (or at least reducing) 
the need to purchase energy from external suppliers but 
also provides the advantage of securing the power sup -
ply in energy crises, for example, in cases of regional or 
national power cuts or when disconnected from the grid 
due to conflict [ 122]. Two studies evaluated the applica -
tion of (hybrid) photovoltaic systems to power health -
care facilities and established their feasibility [ 122, 136]. 
However, both studies were conducted in settings with 
favourable conditions for generating solar energy: Libya 
and the Philippines. Renewable energy generation from 
the most popular technology—photovoltaic panels—is 
weather-and climate-dependent. This challenge can be 
mitigated by having hybrid systems. Furthermore, an ini -
tial (quite substantial) economic investment is necessary, 
although research suggests a return on investment of 20% 
[122]. Exploiting combined energy generation and using 
geothermal energy, hydrogen and biomass for heating 
healthcare facilities are low-carbon alternatives to pollut-
ing coal boilers [52, 113, 126, 128, 133, 137, 142, 145].
The second category encompasses managerial strat -
egies to optimise energy consumption, such as imple -
menting retro-commissioning [ 146] and an (artificial 
intelligence-supported) energy management system [ 31, 
56, 110, 142], as well as ensuring the optimal use of space 
and patient flow [ 61, 91, 128, 141, 147]. Other recom -
mended strategies include monitoring and evaluating 
energy consumption [ 110, 112, 128, 137, 142] and mak -
ing use of energy benchmarking and energy certifications 
[45, 48, 52, 54, 59, 76, 125, 128, 137, 142].
The third category focuses on energy efficiency. It is 
recommended that healthcare facilities are built with 
natural light and ventilation, window shading, thermal 
insulation and automatic doors to avoid heat loss [ 31, 
49, 50, 53, 55, 56, 58– 64, 76, 86, 90, 100, 102, 112, 120, 
123, 128, 137, 141, 142] and efficient, low-carbon heating, 
ventilation and air-conditioning systems [ 31, 46, 48– 50, 
56, 59– 63, 65, 76, 86, 88, 91, 92, 96, 97, 100, 110, 112–
114, 120, 121, 123, 128– 130, 137, 141– 143, 148]. Further 
strategies include installing energy-efficient lighting and 
motion sensors [ 31, 45, 46, 48, 49, 54, 56, 58– 64, 76, 86, 
88, 90, 91, 95, 97, 100, 102, 110, 112– 114, 121, 123– 125, 
128– 130, 133, 134, 137, 141– 144, 149– 154] and invest -
ing in energy-efficient devices [ 48, 49, 56, 60– 63, 85, 86, 
88, 92, 110, 123, 124, 127, 128, 134, 137, 141, 142, 144, 
155], including washing tunnels [ 76, 137, 142]. Energy 
Star ratings help to choose high-efficiency equipment. 
Additional actions include autoconfiguration to the most 
energy-saving settings [ 76, 90] and eliminating unneces -
sary machines from stations [142].
The fourth category centres on staff behaviour. Rec -
ommended strategies to reduce greenhouse gas emis -
sions from energy use include turning off equipment and 
lights when not needed and closing doors [ 31, 42, 46, 48, 
49, 54, 59, 62, 74, 78, 80, 81, 85, 90, 91, 95, 112, 113, 121, 
124, 125, 127– 130, 141– 143, 148, 150, 153– 156], taking 
the stairs instead of elevators [ 54, 112, 142] and reducing 
the use of laminar flow [ 78]. Behavioural interventions, 
such as Operation TSC: ‘turning off equipment, switch -
ing off lights and closing doors’ [ 31] or placing stickers 
on elevators to nudge users to take the stairs [ 142] can 
support individuals in making environmentally conscious 
decisions. Moreover, some authors recommend interdis -
ciplinary collaboration to rethink responsible energy use 
[155], design best practice manuals for staff [ 137] and to 
organise internal competitions to save energy [59].
Saving energy in 2024 is not only necessary to combat 
the climate crisis. Since the Russian invasion of Ukraine, 
many parts of the world, especially Europe, have faced an 
energy crisis. Energy prices rose steeply, and the energy 
supply was uncertain at times [ 157]. There is a surge of 
‘green’ alternatives to fossil fuels, as they can secure the 
generation of affordable and clean energy [157].

Reducing scope 3 emissions: telemedicine and ‘green’ 
travel
Most greenhouse gas emissions from global healthcare 
systems are emitted through scope 3, and travel substan -
tially contributes to these emissions [ 14]. Note that in 
this study, patient and visitor commuting were grouped 
into scope 3 since mitigation measures are similar for 
patient, visitor and employee travel and can indirectly be 
influenced by the healthcare system.
One of the most widely used strategies for lowering 
emissions stemming from patient, visitor and employee 
travel is the implementation of digital technologies. 
Patients can utilise virtual health clinics and telemedi -
cine to receive pre- and post-operative care and special -
ised services [ 31, 42, 45– 47, 49, 50, 53, 56, 58, 61– 63, 76, 
78, 80– 82, 85, 86, 88, 90, 92, 97, 110, 112– 114, 121, 123, 
124, 129, 131, 135, 138, 139, 141, 144, 153, 158– 186] as 
well as general home-based healthcare [ 42, 62, 123, 135, 
139, 187]. Electronic health applications can aid patients 
in disease management and thus reduce excessive hos -
pital visits. For example, Maastricht University Medical 
Centre + has implemented a smart care avatar (‘Molly’) 
for remotely monitoring patients with chronic cardiac 
insufficiency [ 188]. Systematic reviews have shown that 
telemedicine reduces the climate footprint of health -
care systems by reducing greenhouse gas emissions 
that are associated with travel, whereby videoconfer -
ences emit more emissions than telephone consultations 
because they consume more energy [ 189– 191]. Purohit 
et al. concluded that, regardless of the type of technol -
ogy, telemedicine carbon emissions are immaterially low 
compared to emissions from mobile combustion [ 189]. 
These findings align with different studies in the pres -
ent systematic literature review [ 161, 162, 170]. Further-
more, research suggests that telemedicine provides large 
time and cost savings as additional benefits and that rural 
and isolated patients, as well as patients requiring highly 
specialised care, may benefit disproportionally from tele -
medicine [ 190]. Indeed, many studies have emphasised 
the co-benefits of telemedicine, ranging from increased 
equity in access [ 180] over increased attendance and 
engagement [162] to the decongestion of healthcare sys -
tems [173]. In addition, healthcare staff could be granted 
to work from home, if possible [ 48, 52, 56, 62, 150, 171], 
trained in eco-driving [ 49, 148, 168, 184] and urged to 
reduce business travel as much as possible, for example, 
by attending meetings, conferences and educational 
courses online [31, 42, 48, 50, 52, 56, 58, 62, 80, 81, 85, 88, 
90, 113, 123, 131, 134, 135, 141, 142, 148, 156, 159, 168].
Importance should be placed on avoiding potential 
inequities arising from increased electronic health and 
telemedicine. A digital infrastructure, technological 
equipment and personal skills are needed to properly 
participate in teleconsultations. However, these barriers 
are mitigated when consultations take place with local 
healthcare providers at telehealth outpatient clinics [160]. 
Additionally, legislation on data protection and patient 
consent must be followed to ensure data security and 
patient well-being.
Telemedicine has accelerated in the last few years, 
particularly since the COVID-19 pandemic and may 
continue to do so in the coming years. Nevertheless, it 
remains to be seen whether teleconsultations will con -
solidate their position in healthcare provision, especially 
since patients may prefer in-person appointments over 
video consultations outside the COVID-19 pandemic 
[192].
Finally, a possible rebound effect should be considered 
[189]. One should think about what the time and money 
saved through telemedicine are used for instead. Oppor -
tunities arise that could harm the climate. Emissions sav -
ings may be diluted if healthcare providers and patients 
use their secured resources to engage in high-carbon 
activities.
Another promising strategy to reduce patient commut-
ing is minimising the need to travel to healthcare facili -
ties by increasing diagnostic and treatment efficiency. 
For example, by implementing remote point-of-care 
testing [171], reducing false-positive referrals [ 153, 193], 
coordinating appointments efficiently [ 52, 88, 151, 168, 
184, 194], ‘Getting It Right First Time’ [31, 78], reducing 
appointment frequency [168, 184, 194], using global posi-
tioning system-based electronic referral systems [ 168, 
184] and community-based care [ 31, 56, 57, 88, 153]. 
‘Getting It Right First Time’ is a national programme ini-
tiated by NHS England to improve patient treatment and 
care, thereby cutting unnecessary travel to and stays at 
healthcare facilities [195].
The last strategy to lower emissions from travel and 
transportation is to promote green and active modes 
of travel. Patients and employees may be encouraged 
to travel actively by walking and cycling or use greener 
travel options, such as electric (or, in the future, hydro -
carbon) vehicles, public transportation, carpooling or 
healthcare shuttle services [ 31, 42, 45, 46, 48, 49, 52– 54, 
56– 62, 76, 80, 81, 85, 86, 88– 90, 112, 113, 121, 123, 129, 
141, 142, 144, 148, 156, 159, 161, 168, 184]. NHS Eng -
land, for example, tests the use of 100% electric mental 
health response vehicles [196] and the ambulance service 
of Basel in Switzerland recently introduced cargo e-bikes 
in their daily practice [ 197]. Walking and cycling to work 
or appointments are low-cost alternatives to polluting 
vehicles. Reducing travel by car has synergistic effects: 
researchers have observed fewer road traffic accidents 
and increased physical activity, which positively impact 
health and well-being [198, 199].

Reducing scope 3 emissions: decarbonising the supply 
chain
Globally, healthcare systems emit greenhouse gases 
largely indirectly via the global healthcare supply chain 
(scope 3)—71% to be exact [ 14]. These emissions stem 
primarily from medical instruments and equipment, 
inhalers, pharmaceuticals, food and catering, water, con -
struction, information and communications technology 
and overall procurement.
Medical instruments and equipment
Medical instruments and equipment contribute substan -
tially to indirect emissions, in addition to travel, particu -
larly through production, transportation and disposal. 
The 5Rs framework (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Rethink and 
Research) is a guide to zero waste that can be applied to 
reduce waste from the supply chain of healthcare sys -
tems [ 149]. A commonly cited strategy is to minimise 
the climate footprint of purchased goods and services 
and procurement processes. For example, reducing the 
use of single-use disposable devices and equipment and 
by choosing more sustainable and reusable alternatives 
instead [31, 42, 45, 47, 51– 54, 56, 58, 60, 61, 65, 68, 69, 
72, 74, 76– 78, 80, 81, 85, 86, 88– 93, 95, 97, 100, 102, 110, 
112– 114, 124, 127, 129– 131, 138, 141, 142, 148, 150, 
153, 155, 156, 158, 161, 174, 200– 219], protecting medi -
cal items for reuse [ 85, 220] and minimising unnecessary 
product use, overage and waste [ 42, 54, 61, 63, 65, 66, 
72, 74, 78, 80, 81, 86, 91– 93, 95, 97, 100, 102, 110, 112, 
113, 141, 142, 150, 153, 159, 194, 210, 212, 215, 216, 220–
223]. Moreover, it is recommended that medical devices, 
equipment and any other material be properly separated 
and recycled at the end of life [ 31, 42, 45, 47– 49, 51, 53, 
54, 56, 58, 61, 62, 68, 69, 72, 74, 76, 80, 81, 89– 93, 95, 100, 
102, 110, 112, 113, 121, 123, 124, 129, 131, 134, 137, 139, 
140, 142– 144, 149, 150, 152– 156, 159, 169, 200, 202, 204, 
207, 209, 211, 212, 215, 216, 224– 230]. Additionally, sin-
gle-use disposable medical devices may be reprocessed 
and reused, and unused supplies may be repurposed, 
shared within other healthcare facilities or donated [ 31, 
51, 54, 57, 74, 76, 80, 81, 85, 88, 91– 93, 95, 97, 102, 110, 
112, 113, 132, 142, 143, 149, 150, 156, 200, 207, 208, 
211, 214, 216, 220, 231– 234]. Banhidy and Banhidy, for 
example, proposed repurposing sterilised mosquito nets 
for hernia mesh repair in low-and middle-income coun -
tries [149] and van Straten et al. reported that the climate 
footprint of reprocessing and reusing filtering facepiece 
2 face masks five times is more than half of the climate 
footprint of masks used only once [ 234]. Notably, the 
mode of reprocessing highly influences emissions from 
reusing devices [ 235, 236]. Sterilisation via washer dis -
infector systems requires dismantling, precleaning, leak 
testing, cleaning, rinsing, drying, sterilisation, packag -
ing and storage of the reusable device; this process is also 
time-consuming, requires staff and equipment and uses 
more than 150  L of water per cycle [ 236]. The mode of 
sterilisation determines the environmental impact by up 
to 90% [229].
Furthermore, greenhouse gas emissions are highly 
dependent on the energy efficiency of used appliances 
and the use and disposal of personal protective equip -
ment [ 237]. A trade-off must be reached: if single-use 
disposable products are chosen, much waste will be pro -
duced. In contrast, if reusable products are selected or 
single-use disposable products are reprocessed for reuse, 
less waste will be generated, but more energy and water 
will be required for sterilisation [ 229]. Generally, reus -
able products are more climate-friendly than products 
disposed of immediately after use [ 238], but national 
regulations must be checked, and environmental viability 
should be considered individually.
Another commonly cited measure to decarbonise the 
supply chain is to reduce packaging and, if needed, to 
use environmentally friendly packaging [ 31, 45, 48, 53, 
56, 58, 66, 76– 78, 85, 95, 114, 115, 121, 127, 130, 132, 
138– 140, 149, 169, 171, 200, 209, 216, 222]. Other action 
items include switching from single-use bottles to tap 
water [124, 142], composting organic and bio-degradable 
materials [46, 49, 51, 53, 54, 60, 76, 151, 211, 226, 239], 
increasing the lifespan of products [ 52, 55, 76, 86, 131, 
134, 155, 156, 207, 208, 211, 233], adhering to medical 
waste treatment and disposal guidelines [ 137, 224, 240], 
introducing take-back programmes [ 48, 54, 74, 93, 121, 
137, 138, 142, 203, 204, 209, 211, 228], collaborating in 
interdisciplinary teams to discuss waste production [ 142, 
149, 155], centralising waste collection in the hallways 
of healthcare facilities [ 142], conducting regular waste 
audits [ 48, 54, 62, 95, 100, 123, 132, 142, 169, 202, 214, 
216, 220], compacting or shredding ordinary waste [ 137, 
142, 144], using smart waste management systems [ 171] 
and developing a low-carbon waste management net -
work [241].
Inhalers
Inhalers contribute to healthcare’s climate footprint 
because they contain potent greenhouse gases known 
as hydrofluorocarbons, which are used as propellants to 
deliver medication [242]. When these inhalers are used, a 
significant amount of hydrofluorocarbons is released into 
the atmosphere. These gases have a much greater global 
warming potential than carbon dioxide, meaning that 
they trap heat in the atmosphere more effectively. The 
climate footprint of inhalers can be reduced by substitut -
ing metered-dose inhalers with dry-powder or soft mist 
inhalers or alternative treatment strategies whenever 
possible and minimising propellant release [31, 48, 50, 57, 
58, 60, 66, 88– 90, 230, 242– 244].

Pharmaceuticals
While pharmaceuticals play a crucial role in healthcare, 
their production, distribution and disposal can have neg -
ative environmental impacts that contribute to climate 
change. Efforts to minimise these impacts include the 
pursuit of ‘green prescribing’ principles and good man -
agement to reduce pharmaceutical use and waste [ 52, 
54, 56, 58, 63, 66, 78, 81, 88, 89, 102, 121, 141, 210, 245, 
246], improving pharmaceutical properties so that they 
become temperature resistant and last longer [ 66, 228], 
providing multi-dose vials and different sizes [ 65, 66, 85], 
choosing low-carbon pharmaceuticals, if possible [ 247], 
‘greening’ the manufacturing process of pharmaceuticals 
[48, 66], combining primary and secondary packaging at 
one location [66], implementing e-labelling [48], ensuring 
responsible disposal and waste (water) treatment [ 48, 54, 
138, 247] and choosing sustainable options to transport 
pharmaceuticals [228, 248].
Food and catering
Making sustainable food choices can help mitigate the 
environmental impact of food production and contrib -
ute to climate change mitigation efforts, for example, by 
reducing food waste [ 31, 48, 54, 56, 58, 60, 88, 142, 239, 
249], providing fresh, regional and seasonal plant-based 
meals in hospitals [ 31, 46, 49– 54, 56– 58, 60, 76, 88, 113, 
142, 148, 156, 210, 249, 250], purchasing from organic 
and certified producers [ 142, 204], and organising farm -
ers’ markets at hospitals, such as the Sidney Children’s 
Hospital Network [53, 54].
Water, construction and information and communications 
technology
Less known factors contributing to the climate footprint 
of healthcare systems are water usage, construction and 
emissions stemming from information and communica -
tions technology. These can be mitigated by saving and 
recovering water [ 42, 48, 54, 58, 62, 68, 78, 95, 102, 112, 
121, 123, 124, 127, 134, 139, 142– 144, 150, 152, 212, 
251], using aquifer systems, if possible [ 120], considering 
environmental aspects when constructing or renovat -
ing buildings [31, 142, 200, 252] and reducing the energy 
consumption of teleconsultations [ 161] and electronic 
health record software [172].
Sustainable procurement
Sustainable procurement plays a fundamental role in 
mitigating climate change by driving emissions reduc -
tions, promoting sustainable practices, protecting eco -
systems, building resilience and aligning with corporate 
sustainability goals. By integrating sustainability criteria 
into procurement processes, healthcare organisations 
can leverage their purchasing power to drive positive 
environmental outcomes and contribute to global efforts 
to address climate change. Sustainable procurement was 
widely cited for decarbonising the supply chain. The lit -
erature recommends implementing a sustainable pro -
curement scheme and urging vendors to disclose their 
environmental performance [ 31, 48, 49, 51, 54, 56– 62, 
66, 74, 76, 77, 85, 88, 90, 93, 97, 110, 112, 113, 121, 124, 
132, 134, 141, 142, 149, 155, 158, 200, 204, 208, 211, 212, 
220, 247, 253], purchasing locally [ 58, 77, 88, 124, 130, 
131, 149, 168, 200, 222] and in bulk [ 88, 144, 168], re-
evaluating purchasing contracts [51, 60, 74, 92, 112, 204], 
reducing purchasing needs, for example, by planting veg -
etables on a rooftop farm [ 200, 251] or building beehive 
boxes [143, 251], developing models for low-carbon sup -
ply chain networks [ 48, 60, 66, 132, 149, 193, 254– 261], 
advocating for a circular economy [52, 53, 55, 60, 66, 208, 
211, 234] and assisting and incentivising manufacturers 
to become more sustainable [ 48, 51, 52, 61, 66, 133, 229]. 
Some healthcare facilities implement creative solutions. 
The Holy Name Medical Center set up beehives to pro -
duce honey locally for eating or creating lip balms and 
skin care products, and the Boston Medical Center built 
a rooftop farm to harvest vegetables [ 251]. In this way, 
fewer products need to be procured via the supply chain.
Co-benefits
Increasing green spaces, planting trees and vegetables, 
and building beehives have multiple co-benefits. On the 
one hand, they contribute to protecting biodiversity, 
which is essential for the functioning of ecosystems and 
climate regulation [ 4, 6]. On the other hand, some stud -
ies suggest that green and blue spaces positively impact 
physical and mental health [ 262, 263]. Moreover, plant -
ing vegetables on healthcare facility premises or organ -
ising farmers’ markets facilitates the provision of fresh, 
locally sourced and sustainable food. Several authors rec -
ommend that hospitals serve vegetarian and plant-based 
meals and reduce dairy and meat consumption associ -
ated with high carbon emissions. This is a particularly 
appealing solution because plant-based meals provide 
several health benefits [264, 265].
‘Greening’ clinical practices, planetary health education 
and system change
Healthcare provision has become increasingly complex, 
technologically advanced and resource-intensive [ 266]. 
An effective strategy to reduce the climate footprint of 
healthcare is to upsurge outpatient [ 31, 63, 86, 95, 141], 
non-invasive and non-robotic procedures [ 47, 61, 63, 72, 
78, 86, 92, 95, 110, 113, 115, 124, 141, 150, 207, 215, 223] 
and to minimise the production of personalised materials 
with no added value for patients [267]. Innovative robotic 
procedures may be beneficial for surgeons; however, a 
systematic review by Papadopoulou et al. revealed that 
the environmental impact of robotic surgery outweighs

the clinical benefit and should therefore be used scarcely 
[268]. Another issue is overdiagnosis and overtesting. 
Unnecessary imaging and laboratory tests contribute 
to direct and indirect greenhouse emissions and should 
be reduced to a clinically valuable minimum [ 47, 50, 52,

169, 205, 207, 269– 276], including duplicate work [ 63, 
156]. ‘Choosing Wisely’ is a campaign from the American 
Board of Internal Medicine Foundation initiating discus -
sions between healthcare providers and patients about 
the necessity of tests, treatments and procedures [277].
Other action items associated with clinical practices 
that affect multiple scopes include utilising artificial 
intelligence [ 61, 153, 171, 246], choosing reprocessing 
methods with the lowest climate footprint and optimis -
ing the reprocessing process [ 42, 110, 123, 127, 140, 213, 
229, 235], reducing paper consumption, particularly by 
digitalisation [31, 49, 50, 54, 62, 85, 90, 92, 112, 113, 121,

211, 247], utilising three-dimensional printing [ 72, 171] 
and adequately managing stock and inventory [74, 80, 92,

Educating healthcare professionals about the climate 
change and health nexus and healthcare’s contribution 
to the climate crisis is essential. Multiple authors have 
called for the integration of planetary health and sustain -
able healthcare provision in medical curricula, forming a 
new generation of sustainable and climate-smart health -
care workers [50, 66, 86, 124, 141, 152, 158, 210, 247, 253, 
279– 287].
Moreover, organising hackathons to find innovative 
solutions to healthcare system decarbonisation [ 149, 
171], implementing possibilities for knowledge exchange 
between healthcare workers from high-income and low-
and middle-income countries (‘twinning’) [149] and set -
ting up Green Teams to raise awareness and implement 
sustainability interventions [ 42, 51, 53, 54, 56, 62, 68, 69, 
82, 85, 93, 95, 100, 112, 113, 121, 123, 124, 132, 142, 143, 
210, 212, 216, 220, 247, 288] may be effective emission 
reduction actions. The ATACH is one example of an ini -
tiative promoting knowledge exchange and collaborative 
work to foster low-carbon healthcare systems [27].
Furthermore, studies recommend adhering to environ -
mental standards by the International Standardisation 
Organisation [ 49, 158], participating in decarbonisation 
alliances and initiatives to collectively work towards a 
shared goal [ 51, 54, 85, 92, 100, 142, 210], applying car -
bon capture technologies, engaging in carbon offset proj -
ects and increasing green spaces [ 31, 48, 134, 142], and 
considering greenhouse gas emissions in organisational 
procedures, such as job descriptions and performance 
evaluations to substantiate decarbonisation efforts [ 52, 
56, 112, 154]. Finally, some authors propose system-level 
changes in healthcare system activities, such as climate 
footprint monitoring and conducting life cycle assess -
ments of products and services to enable the selection 
of better alternatives [ 31, 47– 49, 53, 56, 57, 62, 66, 69, 
74, 76, 80, 81, 85, 86, 88, 91, 93, 112, 113, 123, 124, 137, 
139, 141– 143, 156, 158, 194, 201, 203, 205– 207, 212– 218, 
229, 234– 237, 245, 250, 253, 289– 295], incorporating 
carbon costs into health technology assessments [ 47, 
158, 187, 291, 296], integrating health services, shifting 
towards value-based healthcare and eliminating activity-
based payment schemes [ 55, 76, 82, 158, 211], acceler -
ating health promotion and disease prevention [ 47, 52, 
53, 55, 63, 78, 85, 113, 124, 139, 158, 168, 210, 245] and 
introducing carbon emission caps [ 158]. Moreover, two 
authors called for patient and public involvement [ 124, 
142]. A complete list of personal, organisational, and sys -
tem-level actions for decarbonising healthcare systems,

from the literature, can be found in Additional File 4.
Measuring the climate footprint of healthcare systems
Sustainability interventions may be implemented by 
dedicated working groups, so-called Green Teams. Note 
that it is essential to make evidence-informed recom -
mendations. Therefore, technical, economic and human 
resource feasibility should be determined beforehand 
[212]. Likewise, assessing the climate footprint of prod -
ucts, technologies, practices and services is important to 
justify organisational changes and base decisions to tran -
sition to allegedly climate-friendlier options on evidence. 
It is equally important to evaluate decarbonisation inter -
ventions and determine their effectiveness correctly since 
the most environmentally sustainable choice may not 
always be the expected one [ 229, 235– 237, 295]. Assess-
ing the cradle-to-grave climate footprint of specific prod-
ucts is often unproblematic since validated standardised 
tools exist. The life cycle assessment of practices and 
services poses more of a challenge, for example, as there 
are no standardised methods or validated tools to assess 
the climate footprint of telehealth services [ 297]. Typi-
cally, publicly available (sometimes adapted) carbon cal -
culators are used to determine carbon emissions [ 297]. 
Indeed, the present study confirms this finding. Most 
studies have used unvalidated methods to estimate the 
carbon footprint.
Five studies proposed the inclusion of carbon costs 
and sustainability considerations in health technology 
assessments [47, 158, 187, 291, 296]. Hensher and McAli-
ster et al. demonstrated the feasibility of doing so [ 291, 
296]. In contrast, a scoping review by Pinho-Gomes et 
al. concluded that the lack of disaggregated data about 
single technologies’ environmental impact, together with 
uncertainty about the handling of trade-offs between 
health and the environment, make the integration of 
sustainability considerations into health technology 
Page 15 of 25
Mirow et al. Sustainable Earth Reviews             (2024) 7:28 
assessment and public health and medical guidelines 
challenging [298].
Climate change mitigation and adaptation will likely be 
cost-effective and generate significant health co-benefits 
[299]. The great potential of decarbonisation actions to 
generate substantial positive externalities and create syn -
ergistic benefits for ecosystems and society [ 300] neces -
sitates adding social value, including health co-benefits, 
to the evaluation of mitigation investments. Recognising 
this need, the World Health Organisation developed ‘ A 
framework for the quantification and economic valua -
tion of health outcomes originating from health and non-
health climate change mitigation and adaptation action’ 
[301]. Aligning with the idea of Health in all Policies, 
this newly published economic framework aims to inte -
grate health outcomes into climate policy. Quantifying 
the value of climate action in healthcare and beyond may 
encourage decision-makers to ‘step up their game’ and 
fully commit to decarbonising healthcare systems.
The future: artificial intelligence to decarbonise 
healthcare?
Artificial intelligence will arguably shape the future of 
healthcare. Publications from this systematic literature 
review discussed the application of artificial intelligence 
to support laboratory work [171], triage patients in emer-
gency rooms [246], detect emerging outbreaks with novel 
symptom patterns [ 246], aid diagnosis [ 153] and deliver 
remote care [ 61]. Other studies have proposed enhanc -
ing drones [ 44] and energy-management systems [ 56] 
with artificial intelligence. Using artificial intelligence in 
healthcare has the potential to save resources and thereby 
contribute to decarbonisation efforts. However, the life 
cycle greenhouse gas emissions of artificial intelligence 
technologies cannot be disregarded. According to Richie, 
artificial intelligence programming, development and 
use are energy-and resource-intensive, thereby forming a 
considerable climate footprint [246]. Contradicting opin-
ions about the environmental sustainability of artificial 
intelligence applications in healthcare exist, illustrating a 
need for further research.
It is essential to note that pioneering decarbonisation 
strategies exist that were not discussed in this paper. 
‘Greening’ chemical and industrial processes can contrib-
ute to lowering indirect scope 3 emissions of healthcare 
systems and may significantly impact their emissions in 
the future.
Strengths and limitations
The findings of this systematic literature review widely 
align with results from previous studies [ 32, 34, 302] 
and recommendations from key stakeholders, such as 
the World Health Organisation [ 11] and Health Care 
Without Harm [ 15], but add more detail and scope to 
reviewed decarbonisation actions, including conven -
tional and unique approaches to climate-friendly health -
care and important considerations and recommendations 
for implementation (see Additional File  4). Among all 
identified decarbonisation actions, 104 can be classified 
as ‘unique, ’ as they have been studied or recommended 
by three papers or fewer. These novel and less-researched 
strategies span all emission scopes and focus on various 
areas, from smart technology and novel agents to highly 
effective and resourceful appointment scheduling and 
referral systems. Moreover, this study underlines the pro-
posed recommendations with evidence from 96 empiri -
cal studies. Actions were grouped into Greenhouse Gas 
Protocol scopes 1, 2 and 3 to explore interventions tar -
geted at emissions originating from various activities of a 
healthcare system.
One strength of the present study is the inclusion of 
non-research literature. Including literature beyond 
empirical studies enabled the collection of a compre -
hensive list of decarbonisation actions, including unique 
solutions not assessed in primary studies. Addition -
ally, this systematic literature review was not limited to 
a particular field within healthcare, which made it pos -
sible to identify solutions across specialities and to cover 
essentially all areas within healthcare, offering a holistic 
approach to decarbonisation efforts.
Searching for literature across multiple domains related 
to healthcare and not limiting the search to specific study 
designs and types of literature mitigated the problem of 
needing more literature to conduct a meaningful system -
atic literature review which was a problem cited in the 
literature [150, 303]. Lastly, the high level of detail con -
cerning decarbonisation actions across all Greenhouse 
Gas Protocol scopes may be helpful for healthcare per -
sonnel and decision-makers who seek more information 
beyond official guidance documents that are often lim -
ited in their provision of detail. We created a repository 
which ideally offers practitioners straight-forward entry 
points to decarbonise healthcare systems.
The present systematic literature review has its limita -
tions. First, the time and resources for conducting this 
systematic literature review were restricted: a single 
researcher was conducting the research as part of a uni -
versity undergraduate dissertation. Second, the value of a 
literature review always depends on the quality of exist -
ing studies. In the present case, the body of research 
evidence on healthcare decarbonisation approaches 
was very diverse and of varying quality—a considerable 
amount of non-research literature was included. More -
over, despite utilising a comprehensive search strategy, 
it is possible that not all relevant studies were identified 
since sustainable healthcare is a highly interdisciplin -
ary matter, and searches were limited to the timespan of 
2018–2023, studies published in English and listed in the 
Page 16 of 25
Mirow et al. Sustainable Earth Reviews             (2024) 7:28 
PubMed/MEDLINE, GreenFILE, Business Source Com -
plete and CINAHL databases. Thus, relevant searches 
might have been overlooked, even though backward 
citation searching (snowballing) may have attenuated 
this limitation. Additionally, not all potentially pertinent 
publications could be retrieved in full because of access 
restrictions. Finally, knowledge and approaches to 
healthcare system decarbonisation advance rapidly. After 
the literature search was completed, several new studies 
were published that were not included in this review.
In addition to potential limitations in the review pro -
cess, there is a risk of bias resulting from the included 
literature, such as selection, measurement, confound -
ing, information, publication, reporting and confirma -
tion biases, and ‘Greenwashing’ from industry-supported 
papers. The publications vary in their study designs and 
methodologies. For example, deviations in system bound-
aries exist. The individual limitations and methodologi -
cal differences between the publications (including their 
degrees of reliability and validity) may limit the compa -
rability and generalisability of the findings. Neverthe -
less, the study did not aim to quantify the effectiveness of 
decarbonisation efforts (see Blom et al. [ 35]). Instead, it 
aimed to examine various actions that can be undertaken 
to lower greenhouse gas emissions of healthcare systems 
worldwide. Therefore, the possible heterogeneous find -
ings of the studies were not considered a disadvantage.
Recommendations for research, policy and practice
Having reviewed decarbonisation actions at the personal, 
organisational and system levels, the following measures 
related to research, policy and practice seem necessary to 
drive action.
Recommendations for research
This review identifies a diverse range of decarbonisa -
tion actions in healthcare. Strategies to lower emis -
sions related to transportation, anaesthesia, energy use 
and single-use products seem of particular interest to 
researchers. Future studies should focus on quantifying 
the greenhouse gas emission reductions achieved by indi-
vidual interventions, such as regional and total intrave -
nous anaesthesia, reusable medical devices, telemedicine 
and the application of artificial intelligence in healthcare. 
Additionally, less popular interventions (see Additional 
File  4) should be investigated better. Providing robust 
evidence of the effectiveness of these interventions is 
crucial for guiding healthcare workers and decision-
makers. Moreover, it is essential to continuously monitor 
the global climate footprint of healthcare systems, as “we 
can’t mitigate what we don’t monitor” [294]. Clear com -
munication of the connection between climate change 
and health to healthcare providers and the public is also 
imperative.
Recommendations for policy
Healthcare system decarbonisation must be prioritised 
on the political agenda to drive meaningful system-wide 
change. Policymakers should heed the long-standing calls 
from advocacy groups for a socio-ecological transfor -
mation within healthcare. Collaborative efforts between 
policymakers and practitioners are essential to design 
and implement sustainable reforms for national health -
care systems. Introducing a carbon cap for healthcare 
facilities, pharmaceutical industries and suppliers could 
be a powerful mechanism to accelerate decarbonisation. 
Additionally, companies should be mandated to disclose 
information on the environmental performance and life 
cycle assessments of their products and services, with 
sustainable manufacturers receiving competitive advan -
tages to promote environmentally responsible practices. 
The active involvement of key stakeholders, such as 
experts at the intersection of climate change and health, 
healthcare decision-makers and healthcare staff, is cru -
cial to ensure successful implementation of these policies.
Recommendations for practice
Collaboration within transdisciplinary teams is crucial 
for implementing sustainable practices in the daily opera-
tions of healthcare providers. Healthcare staff—including 
medical personnel, allied health professionals, adminis -
trative and support teams, IT specialists, technical staff  
and researchers—should receive targeted training in sus -
tainability practices relevant to their specific roles. Deter-
mining responsibilities within a healthcare facility can 
be challenging. To address this, we recommend to orga -
nise workshops where teams can collaboratively explore 
all available decarbonisation options and clearly assign 
responsibility for implementing each action. Recognised 
organisations and bodies in the field can facilitate this 
transition by offering a wealth of freely available online 
resources, as well as consultancy and training services 
globally. It is also important to identify and train front -
runners who can lead the change in promoting sustain -
able practices within their organisations. Encouraging 
healthcare professionals to become ‘low-carbon health -
care providers’ can be achieved by developing clear, prac-
tical and transparent guidelines and policies, along with 
introducing incentive and reward structures to support 
and motivate sustainable behaviours.
Recommendations for healthcare reform
A key recommendation is the shift towards integrated 
and value-based healthcare, which prioritises preven -
tion over cure, minimises overdiagnosis and overtreat -
ment, moves away from activity-based payment models 
and promotes planetary health. This approach can sig -
nificantly reduce healthcare’s climate footprint, as most 
greenhouse gas emissions stem from healthcare system 
Page 17 of 25
Mirow et al. Sustainable Earth Reviews             (2024) 7:28 
activities. By reducing these activities, energy consump -
tion, anaesthetic emissions, health-related travel and 
waste would logically decrease. Charlesworth et al. also 
highlight essential strategies for addressing the climate 
impact of health organisations, including the impor -
tance of implementation, multi-level leadership, secur -
ing resources, data collection on emissions, system-level 
reforms such as telemedicine and value-based care and 
fostering political awareness and effective engagement 
[304]. These lessons align with the findings of this study, 
underscoring their importance in the pursuit of sustain -
able healthcare.
Additionally, it is exceptionally important to decar -
bonise fairly. Inequalities and disparities within and 
across countries exist—the burden of climate change is 
not equally shared among societies, nor is the burden of 
reducing emissions [ 305]. Healthcare systems must con -
tribute to a just transition towards climate sustainability. 
Moreover, no trade-off should be made between excel -
lent health services and sustainability. Likewise, it must 
be noted that healthcare systems around the world dif -
fer—from private health systems to Bismarck-like health 
insurance systems to Beveridge-like national health 
insurance systems. Therefore, implementing decarboni -
sation actions may be challenging in some healthcare 
systems and special attention should be given to the 
transferability and adaptability of interventions.
Conclusions and outlook
Modern human activities generate environmental dis -
ruptions, including climate change, that adversely affect 
public health. Highly developed healthcare systems are 
needed to meet the rising demand for health services 
related to the climate crisis, consequently provoking a 
rise in healthcare activities and related expenditures. In 
turn, healthcare systems emit increasing greenhouse gas 
emissions further contributing to the climate crisis. But 
good news is, this paradox can be solved with smart plans 
for decarbonisation and original solutions, such as those 
presented in this study. This systematic literature review 
explored potential actions to lower greenhouse gas emis -
sions of healthcare systems globally and aimed to identify 
what it will take to make the decarbonisation of health -
care systems work. Practical actions at the personal and 
organisational levels targeted at transportation, anaesthe-
sia, energy, travel, the supply chain and clinical practices 
can be supported by promoting the planetary health lit -
eracy of healthcare workers, patients and their caregiv -
ers and system changes that need to be implemented at 
a higher level. This review emphasises the critical role 
of transdisciplinary cooperation in achieving healthcare 
decarbonisation, involving collaboration among medical 
staff, allied health professionals, administrative and sup -
port staff, IT and technical teams, as well as researchers 
and educators. Additionally, it provides specific recom -
mendations for conserving resources, such as anaesthetic 
gases, medication and medical equipment and under -
scores the importance of patient and public involvement, 
along with standardisation to best practices.
Transitioning to climate-friendly materials and prac -
tices, combined with a reduction in consumption and 
excessive activities may not be the magic bullet to solve 
the climate crisis but may very well be a meaningful step 
in the right direction towards sustainable, low-carbon 
healthcare systems. This can be achieved by actions 
encompassing all levels of society and looking beyond the 
borders of health systems. Healthcare workers are agents 
of change and are vital in attaining sustained transfor -
mation. Countries may benefit from global knowledge 
exchange, sharing best-practice examples and learnings. 
Looking forward, action plans and audits at the local 
level combined with large-scale measures and policies 
are needed to influence the system as a whole.
